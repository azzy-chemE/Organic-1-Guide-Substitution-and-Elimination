§3 — Mechanism Steps
SN1
Substitution · Nucleophilic · Unimolecular
  1. Ionization (slow, RDS): LG leaves heterolytically; bonding pair goes with LG. Forms planar carbocation + LG⁻. Polar protic solvent stabilizes both ions.
  2. [Optional] Rearrangement: If adjacent C has H or alkyl that would give a more stable carbocation → 1,2-hydride shift or 1,2-methyl shift occurs. Check: is there a more stable carbo+ possible?
  3. [Optional] Proton transfer: If carbocation is adjacent to H that can move to give resonance-stabilized cation, this may occur.
  4. Nucleophilic attack (fast): Nucleophile attacks planar carbocation from both faces equally → racemic mixture (50/50 R/S). If only one face is blocked → partial inversion with some retention.
  5. [Optional] Deprotonation: If nucleophile attacked O to give oxonium ion (R-OH₂⁺), solvent removes proton to give final alcohol.
Key Conditions
3° substrate polar protic solvent weak Nuc good LG low/room temp
🔑 Rearrangement check (SN1/E1): After RDS, look at carbocation. Is there a β-hydrogen or β-alkyl on a carbon adjacent to the + charge that would create a 3° or resonance-stabilized carbocation? If yes, draw the shift and use the rearranged carbocation for subsequent steps.
SN2
Substitution · Nucleophilic · Bimolecular
  1. Backside attack (single concerted step): Nucleophile approaches 180° from the LG. The transition state is a pentacoordinate trigonal bipyramidal species with partial bonds to both Nuc and LG.
  2. Bond formation / bond breaking simultaneous: As Nuc–C bond forms, C–LG bond breaks. No intermediate.
  3. Inversion of configuration (Walden inversion): The three substituents on C invert like an umbrella flipping in wind. If stereocenter is present: R → S or S → R at that carbon.
Key Conditions
CH₃ or 1° (best) 2° (slower) polar aprotic solvent strong Nuc good LG
🔑 Steric effects: CH₃X fastest → 1° → 2° much slower → 3° impossible (blocked). Neopentyl (1° but 3° β-carbon) also very slow. Polar aprotic solvents liberate "naked" anions → much faster SN2 than protic solvents (which cage the nucleophile via H-bonding).
E1
Elimination · Unimolecular
  1. Ionization (slow, RDS): Same first step as SN1! LG leaves to form carbocation. Rate = k[substrate] only.
  2. [Optional] Rearrangement: Exactly as SN1 — check for hydride or alkyl shift to form more stable carbocation.
  3. Deprotonation (fast): Base (often solvent) removes a β-proton from C adjacent to carbocation. π bond forms between α-C and β-C as electrons from C–H bond fill the empty p orbital.
  4. Product: Alkene. Regiochemistry follows Zaitsev — most substituted (most stable) alkene preferred.
Key Conditions
3° substrate polar protic solvent weak base (H₂O, ROH) higher temp good LG
🔑 E1 vs SN1: Both share the same RDS (ionization). Product ratio depends on whether Nuc attacks C⁺ (→SN1) or base removes β-H (→E1). Higher temp and more β-H available → more E1. E1 stereochemistry is NOT stereospecific (carbocation is sp² but rotation around α–β bond is free, so any H can be removed).
E2
Elimination · Bimolecular
  1. Anti-periplanar alignment required: The β-H and LG must be 180° apart (anti-periplanar). In Newman projection: H and LG are anti to each other. This is a geometric requirement — the molecule must adopt this conformation.
  2. Concerted (single step): Base abstracts β-H, electrons flow to form C=C π bond, LG departs simultaneously. Transition state has partial bonds everywhere.
  3. Product stereochemistry determined by which β-H is anti to LG: Check Newman projection. The specific H removed and its spatial relationship to substituents determines whether E or Z alkene is formed.
Key Conditions
strong base bulky base → Hofmann 2° or 3° preferred good LG higher temp
🔑 Anti-periplanar = STEREOSPECIFIC: Only the H that is anti to the LG can be removed. Draw Newman projection, find which H is 180° from LG, and that determines: (a) which carbon loses H → regiochemistry, (b) which face eliminates → E vs Z geometry. Different starting stereoisomers give different alkene products.
§4 — Stereochemistry Details

SN1 Stereochemistry

Type
Racemization (non-stereospecific)
Why?
Planar sp² carbocation — Nuc attacks from either face equally → 50% R + 50% S
In practice
Often slight excess of inversion product because LG leaves from one face and partially blocks it momentarily (ion pair)
Chiral product?
Only if starting material was chiral AND all products are chiral — then racemic mixture

SN2 Stereochemistry

Type
Stereospecific — complete inversion
Why?
Backside (180°) attack only — Walden inversion
R → ?
Depends on CIP priority change. Inversion of spatial arrangement, not necessarily R→S. If priorities change, could be R→R!
Newman tip
Draw product with every group inverted relative to starting material at the reaction center
Multiple stereocenters
Only the attacked center inverts — all other stereocenters retain configuration

E1 Stereochemistry

Type
NOT stereospecific
Why?
Carbocation can rotate freely; any β-H can be removed from any face
Alkene geometry
Zaitsev product; mixture of E/Z possible but thermodynamically more stable E (trans) usually predominates
Regiochemistry
Zaitsev rule: remove H from the β-carbon with fewer H's (gives more substituted alkene)

E2 Stereochemistry

Type
Stereospecific — anti-periplanar required
Requirement
β-H and LG must be 180° (anti) in the transition state → draw Newman projection to verify
E vs Z
Determined by which conformer places H anti to LG. The groups gauche to each other in the anti-periplanar TS end up cis (Z) in product
Cyclohexanes
Both H and LG must be diaxial (trans-diaxial) to achieve anti-periplanar. Draw chair conformation!
Mnemonic
"Bases are anti-social" → anti elimination
§5 — Regiochemistry (Elimination Products)

📐 Zaitsev Rule (most substituted alkene)

When: Small/strong base (NaOH, NaOEt, KOH) or E1 conditions


Rule: Remove the β-H from the carbon bearing fewer hydrogens → gives the alkene with more substituents on the double bond → more stable (more hyperconjugation / less steric strain in product)


Stability order of alkenes:
tetrasubstituted > trisubstituted > disubstituted > monosubstituted > ethylene


Example: 2-bromobutane + NaOEt → major product is 2-butene (trisubstituted) over 1-butene (monosubstituted)

🏔 Hofmann Rule (least substituted alkene)

When: Bulky base (KOtBu, LDA) — base cannot reach the more hindered β-carbon


Rule: Remove H from the carbon bearing more hydrogens (more accessible) → less substituted alkene (Hofmann product)


Also applies to: Quaternary ammonium salts (Hofmann elimination via E2 with ⁻OH), bulky sulfonate LGs


Example: 2-bromo-2-methylbutane + KOtBu → major product is 2-methylbut-1-ene (less substituted) instead of 2-methylbut-2-ene

§6 — E / Z Configuration of Alkene Products

Determining E / Z from E2

Step 1: Draw Newman projection looking down β–α bond (β in front).
Step 2: Rotate to place LG and H anti (180°). Write down what groups are gauche.
Step 3: Groups that are gauche to each other in the anti-periplanar TS will be cis (same side) in the alkene product.
Step 4: Assign E/Z using CIP rules: higher priority on same side = Z; opposite sides = E.

CIP priority: higher atomic number = higher priority. Tie: look at next atoms (like isotopes). Double bond duplicated atoms.

E / Z Quick Rules

E (Entgegen = "opposite") → higher priority groups on opposite sides of double bond.
Z (Zusammen = "together") → higher priority groups on same side.

Trans ≈ E and Cis ≈ Z — but not always! Use CIP rules to be certain.

Thermodynamics: E (trans) usually more stable than Z (cis) due to steric interactions of large groups — so E1 tends to give more E product.

1,2-Hydride Shift

When: Carbocation has adjacent C–H where the H migration would give a more stable carbocation.
What moves: H atom (with its bonding electrons) migrates from β-carbon to the carbocation center.
Result: Carbocation moves one carbon over. Example: 2° → 3° is very favorable.
Arrows: Draw curved arrow from C–H bond → empty p orbital of carbocation.

1,2-Methyl (Alkyl) Shift

When: No adjacent H available, but adjacent C–C bond migration gives more stable carbocation (less common than hydride).
What moves: Entire methyl/alkyl group with its bonding electrons migrates.
Result: Carbon skeleton changes! Product may have rearranged skeleton.
Tip: Always check if rearrangement is possible before drawing product — the major product may be the rearranged one.

§10 — Newman Projection Guide for E2 (& SN2 backside)
Drawing the Newman:
1. Identify α-C (bearing LG) and β-C (bearing H to be removed).
2. Draw Newman looking from β → α (or α → β consistently).
3. Front circle = nearer carbon, back circle = farther carbon.
4. Place all 3 substituents on front C at 120° intervals, all 3 on back C offset.
Finding anti-periplanar H:
1. Identify LG on back carbon.
2. Rotate front or back carbon until an H on front C is 180° from LG.
3. That's your anti-periplanar H → E2 removes it.
4. The substituents gauche to each other at that conformation = cis in alkene product.
Cyclohexane special case:
1. Draw chair conformation.
2. H and LG must both be axial (trans-diaxial) to be anti-periplanar.
3. If they are equatorial/axial mixed → need ring flip first!
4. After ring flip, check which trans-diaxial pair is possible → that determines product.
§11 — Temperature, Heat, and Quick Decision Tips
↑ Higher Temperature
Favors elimination (E2 and E1) over substitution. Entropy-driven — elimination produces 2 molecules (alkene + HX). Use heat to push toward alkene products.
Room Temperature / Cold
Favors substitution (SN1 and SN2). Lower temperature slows elimination. Good for SN2 with good nucleophile in polar aprotic.
Concentration of Base/Nuc
High [base] → E2. Low [base/nuc] → SN1/E1 (depends on substrate). SN2 rate linear in [Nuc] — double concentration, double rate.
SN1 Checklist
  • 3° substrate (or allylic/benzylic)
  • Polar protic solvent
  • Weak/no nucleophile
  • Good LG (Br, I, OTs)
  • Room temp or lower
  • Check for rearrangement!
  • Product = racemic
SN2 Checklist
  • CH₃X or 1° (best); 2° ok
  • Polar aprotic solvent
  • Strong, unhindered nucleophile
  • Good LG
  • Low steric hindrance
  • Product = inverted stereocenter
  • No rearrangement
E1 Checklist
  • 3° substrate
  • Polar protic solvent
  • Weak base (H₂O, ROH)
  • Higher temperature
  • Good LG
  • Check for rearrangement!
  • Zaitsev product (most subst.)
E2 Checklist
  • Any degree (1°,2°,3° ok)
  • Strong base
  • Bulky base → Hofmann
  • Small base → Zaitsev
  • Anti-periplanar H required
  • Draw Newman projection
  • Stereospecific E/Z product
§12 — Activating Poor Leaving Groups

OH → OTs / OMs

Convert alcohol to tosylate (TsCl/py) or mesylate (MsCl/py). The sulfonate is an excellent leaving group. Stereospecific: configuration retained at carbon during tosylation, then inverted by SN2 if desired.

OH → H₂O (SN1 conditions)

Protonate OH with H⁺ (strong acid, H₂SO₄ or HX) → H₂O becomes the leaving group (neutral, excellent LG). Then SN1 or E1 proceeds. Used for alcohol substitution and dehydration.

NH₂ → N₂ (Diazotization)

Primary amines + NaNO₂/HCl → diazonium salt (R–N₂⁺). N₂ gas is the best leaving group. Used for SN1/aromatic substitution. Aliphatic diazonium → immediate SN1 + rearrangement.
